§ Preface

Frame and scope

Alpine skiing admits at least three different framings: a technical practice with a codified vocabulary, a phenomenology of moving through resistant terrain, and a high-risk activity with a well-characterized injury epidemiology. The three frames don't reduce to each other, and most confusion about what skiing is comes from running them together.

The technical spine of what follows is the Professional Ski Instructors of America (PSIA) Skills Concept and its current articulation as the Five Fundamentals of Skiing — the most coherent technical vocabulary the sport has produced. Around that spine sit notes on equipment geometry, snow taxonomy, the philosophy of bodily skill, and the safety literature.

What follows is a working set of notes — half wiki, half commonplace book — added to as occasion arises. Sections are at varying stages of completeness.

§ 序言

框架与范围

高山滑雪至少容纳三种不同的框架：一套有编码化术语的技术实践、一种穿行于阻力地形的身体现象学、以及一项有明确流行病学刻画的高风险活动。三者互不还原；关于滑雪是什么的多数混淆，都来自把它们混为一谈。

下文的技术脊柱是美国职业滑雪教练协会（PSIA）的 Skills Concept（技能体系）及其当前表述——Five Fundamentals of Skiing（五大基本功），是这项运动迄今最连贯的一套技术语言。围绕这条脊柱，配以装备几何、雪的分类、身体技能的哲学、以及安全文献的笔记。

下面是一份工作笔记——半是 wiki，半是札记本——随时增补。各章成熟度不一。

I.

The Five Fundamentals

PSIA's Five Fundamentals of Skiing are the centerpiece of the National Standards — five sentences that, together, describe what the organization considers good skiing. They are deliberately abstract: no specific technique, no specific terrain, no specific skier. The idea is that the same five describe a beginner wedge turn and a World Cup giant slalom run, only at different magnitudes and blends.

The fundamentals descend from an older framework called the Skills Concept, introduced by the US Demonstration Team at Interski 1975 in Štrbské Pleso, Czechoslovakia. The Skills Concept inventories every movement that affects a ski's behavior into three (sometimes four) categories — rotary, edging, pressure control, with balance as the substrate. The Five Fundamentals, in their current wording, answer a different question on top of that inventory: given the Skills Concept, what does great skiing look like?

PSIA-AASI was founded in 1961 from a unification of regional associations. The American Technique made its debut at Interski 1975 and earned a multi-page spread in Sports Illustrated. A near-identical Skills framework has since been adopted by most other alpine teaching nations.

Each fundamental below is given first in PSIA's official wording, then in plain biomechanical terms, with the typical error pattern noted and a teaching cue.

1

Fore–Aft Balance

Control the relationship of the center of mass to the base of support to direct pressure along the length of the skis.

In ordinary terms: balance is maintained front-to-back, and that balance directs where on the ski pressure is being applied. The fronts of the skis turn (their sidecut bends them); the middle is stable; the tails skid. A centered skier can direct pressure forward to engage the tip; a skier behind the boots cannot, regardless of effort.

The most common error in skiing — at every level — is being too far back. Fear produces it; steep terrain produces it; fatigue produces it. The skier feels safer because the body sits "behind" the danger; the ski performs worse because the tip stops working. The result is a skier braking against their own equipment.

Shins forward against the boot tongue, all the way down. Pressure on the tongue means the stance is correct.
2

Lateral Balance & the Outside Ski

Control pressure from ski to ski and direct pressure toward the outside ski.

A turn is asymmetric. The outside ski (the downhill ski once the turn is shaped) carries most of the load — it has the longer lever arm against the snow and the better edge angle. Mikaela Shiffrin's right turn is a left-foot turn; her left turn is a right-foot turn. This holds for every good skier. It does not hold for most beginners, who weight both skis equally, or weight the inside ski.

The inside ski is not passive — it tips and steers in concert — but it carries less. Equal-weighting both feet limits what the outside ski can do, and tends to produce skidded, unpredictable turns.

Find the little toe of the outside foot and stand on it.
3

Edge Angle — Inclination & Angulation

Control edge angles through a combination of inclination and angulation.

Two ways to put a ski on edge. Inclination: lean the whole body into the turn, like a bicycle around a corner. Angulation: bend at the hip and the knee so the lower body tips into the turn while the upper body stays more upright. Real skiing uses both, blended to the speed and the radius of the turn.

Beginners over-incline (they lean and then fall to the inside). Intermediates often only incline (they "A-frame" because they have nowhere to put the inside leg). Advanced skiers angulate at the hip — where the sidecut comes alive. The classic drill is the pole-drag: drag both pole tips on the snow through the turn. Keeping them in contact requires dropping the inside hip and shoulder up, pushing the outside hip down — angulation made visible.

Inside hand light, outside hand heavy. Inside hip up, outside hip down.
4

Rotary — Turning the Legs Under a Quiet Upper Body

Control the skis' rotation (turning, pivoting, steering) with leg rotation, separate from the upper body.

The legs rotate in the hip socket — the femur turning under a stable pelvis. The upper body faces, roughly, down the fall line on shorter turns and across the slope on longer ones, but it never drives the turn by twisting. This is called upper-lower body separation, and it is largely absent in most skiers below the advanced level.

The instinct, especially in difficult terrain, is to throw the shoulders into the turn. It feels powerful. It is wrong: shoulder-driven turns destabilize balance and prevent the femurs from doing their job. The standard remedy is pivot slips: ski directly down the fall line, pivot the skis 90° to each side while keeping the upper body absolutely still and pointed straight down.

Belly button down the fall line. Feet do the work.
5

Pressure Management — the Underrated One

Regulate the magnitude of pressure created through ski/snow interaction.

Where the first four fundamentals are about direction — fore–aft, side-to-side, edge, rotation — the fifth is about magnitude. How much force is the ski putting into the snow? When? Pressure builds in a turn (the centripetal load increases as edge angle and speed do) and releases between turns. Skiers regulate this through flexion and extension — bending and rising at the ankle, knee, and hip — and through fore–aft and lateral movements that distribute pressure across the ski.

PSIA examiner Ann Schorling has called this "the underrated fifth fundamental." It is what makes skiing feel like skiing. Two skiers with identical edge angles and stances can produce wildly different turns based on how they regulate pressure — one carving cleanly, one chattering. In powder, the stance stays tall and supple to spread pressure; on ice, the ankle rolls hard to concentrate it. The fifth fundamental is the one that turns technique into touch.

Ask first what the snow wants. Then your skis. Then yourself.
The Skills Concept's three categories — Rotary, Edging, Pressure — line up roughly with Fundamentals 4, 3, and 5. Fundamentals 1 and 2 are the balance substrate that all of them rest on. The match isn't perfect, and PSIA explicitly resists making it so.
I.

五大基本功

PSIA 的 五大基本功（Five Fundamentals of Skiing）是其国家标准的核心——五句话，合起来描述了这个组织所认为的"好的滑雪"。它们故意写得抽象：不指向某种具体技术、某种具体地形、某种具体水平的滑雪者。其用意是同一组五条同时描述初学者的犁式转弯和世界杯大回转，只是在量级和配比上不同。

五大基本功源自一个更早的框架——Skills Concept（技能体系），由美国示范队在 1975 年捷克斯洛伐克 Štrbské Pleso 的 Interski 大会上首次推出。Skills Concept 把所有影响滑板行为的动作归入三类（有时四类）：rotary（旋转）、edging（立刃）、pressure control（压力控制），以 balance（平衡）为底层。五大基本功现行的措辞，回答的是"在 Skills Concept 之上，什么叫好的滑雪？"

PSIA-AASI 成立于 1961 年，由几个地区性协会合并而成。"美国技术（American Technique）"在 1975 年 Interski 大会上首次亮相，并在《体育画报》上获得多页报道。一套近乎相同的 Skills 框架后来被绝大多数高山滑雪教学国家采纳。

下面每条先给出 PSIA 的官方措辞，再以朴素的生物力学语言展开，记录典型错误模式，并附一句教学口令。

1

前后平衡

控制重心与支撑面的关系，使压力沿滑板长轴方向分布。
Control the relationship of the center of mass to the base of support to direct pressure along the length of the skis.

用人话讲：保持前后方向上的平衡，并用这个平衡选择把压力施加在滑板的哪个部分。滑板前段负责转弯（板的侧切几何让它在压力下弯曲），中段稳定，尾段会侧滑。一个站位居中的滑雪者可以把压力前移、激活板头；一个重心后坐的滑雪者再用力也做不到，只是徒劳。

滑雪里最普遍的错误——所有水平段都一样——是身体太靠后。恐惧把人推到那里，陡坡把人推到那里，疲劳把人推到那里。学生感觉这样更安全，因为身体在"危险"的后方；可是滑板的反馈变得更糟，因为板头开始失灵。最终结果是滑雪者在用自己的装备给自己刹车。

小腿胫骨始终顶住雪靴前舌——从头到尾都顶住。如果靴舌有压力，你就在该在的位置。
2

左右平衡与外侧脚

控制压力在两板之间的分配，使压力指向外侧板。
Control pressure from ski to ski and direct pressure toward the outside ski.

转弯是不对称的。外侧板（弯成形之后位于山下方的那一只）承担大部分负荷——它对雪面有更长的力臂、更好的立刃角度。Mikaela Shiffrin 的右转是一次左脚转弯，左转是一次右脚转弯。所有优秀滑雪者都如此；而大多数初学者不是——他们均匀分配两板压力，更糟的还会压在内侧板上。

内侧板并不是被动的——它同步立刃、同步引导——只是承受较少。两脚均匀施压会限制外侧板能做的事情，倾向于产生侧滑、不可控的转弯。

找到外侧脚的小拇指，站到它上面去。
3

立刃角度——倾斜与折屈

通过倾斜（inclination）与折屈（angulation）的组合控制立刃角度。
Control edge angles through a combination of inclination and angulation.

把滑板立起来有两种方式。倾斜（inclination）：整个身体向弯心倾倒，像自行车过弯。折屈（angulation）：在髋关节和膝关节处折叠，下半身向弯心倾，上半身保持相对直立。真实的滑雪两者并用，按速度和半径配比。

初学者过度倾斜（一斜就向内倒地）。中级常常只倾斜（出现"A 字腿"，因为内侧腿无处安放）。高级滑雪者在髋部折屈——侧切几何在这一刻才真正激活。经典练习是 pole-drag（拖杖）：转弯过程中两根雪杖的杖尖始终擦着雪面。要做到这一点，必须把内侧的髋和肩抬起、外侧的髋和肩压下——折屈在视觉上被看见。

内手轻、外手重。内髋上、外髋下。
4

旋转——双腿在安静的上身之下转动

用腿的旋转（转向、枢转、引导）控制滑板的旋转，与上半身分离。
Control the skis' rotation (turning, pivoting, steering) with leg rotation, separate from the upper body.

双腿在髋臼里旋转——股骨在稳定的骨盆下方转动。上半身在短弯里大致朝向 fall line（落山线），在长弯里朝向横切方向，但永远不用扭转上身来驱动转弯。这叫 upper-lower body separation（上下分离），这是高级水平之下的多数滑雪者所欠缺的。

本能反应——尤其在难地形上——是把肩膀甩进去。感觉很有力，是错的。肩膀驱动的转弯破坏平衡，让股骨没法做它该做的事。标准的修正是 pivot slip（枢转下滑）：径直沿 fall line 下滑，把双板枢转 90° 到一侧再到另一侧，上身始终绝对静止地正对山下方。

肚脐朝下山方向。脚去做事。
5

压力管理——被低估的那一条

调节由滑板与雪互动产生的压力大小。
Regulate the magnitude of pressure created through ski/snow interaction.

前四条讲方向——前后、左右、立刃、旋转——第五条讲大小。滑板对雪施加多少力？什么时候？转弯过程中压力会增加（向心载荷随立刃角度和速度增大），转弯之间释放。滑雪者通过弯伸（踝、膝、髋的屈伸）以及前后/左右的移动来调节压力，把压力分布在滑板的不同位置。

PSIA 考官 Ann Schorling 把这一条称作"被低估的第五基本功"，她说得对。它就是让滑雪感觉像滑雪的那一条。两个立刃角度、站姿一模一样的滑雪者，会因为压力管理的不同产生天差地别的转弯——一个干净刻入，另一个抖动失控。粉雪里要保持站立柔软，把压力分散开；冰面上要内扣脚踝，把压力集中起来。第五条让"技术"变成"触感（touch）"。

先问雪要什么。再问你的板。最后才是你自己。
Skills Concept 的三大类——Rotary、Edging、Pressure——大致对应基本功 4、3、5。基本功 1 和 2 是它们共同站立的平衡基底。这种对应不是严丝合缝的，PSIA 也明确不这么强求。
II.

A Technical Progression

Skiing technique is usually taught as a ladder — wedge, wedge christie, parallel, advanced parallel, carving. The ladder is fine for a first sketch but it misleads in a couple of ways. It implies linear progression where intermediates often regress in some skills as they push others. It also implies a single endpoint, when in fact the top of the ladder branches: carving and short-radius pivot turns are sibling skills, neither subordinate to the other.

The diagram tries to capture several points:

  • The first three stages are roughly linear. A wedge teaches the foot what an edge feels like; the wedge christie introduces outside-ski dominance; intermediate parallel makes the skis behave together. Skipping any of these usually leaves a gap that resurfaces later under stress.
  • Advanced parallel is the branch point. From here, technique can specialize toward carving (where edge angle dominates and the ski cuts a clean arc) or toward short-radius pivot turns (where rotary and pressure regulation dominate and the ski pivots and scrapes the snow). Both require the same fundamentals, blended differently.
  • The two termini are siblings. Carving is not "more advanced" than short-radius turns. They demand different terrain and different snow. A carved turn on a 25° groomer is one thing; a tight bump line demands rotary work and pressure management, not more edge angle. Treating one as the goal and the other as a stepping-stone produces lopsided skiers.
  • The dotted feedback line is real. Short-radius pivot work develops pressure regulation and upper-lower separation in ways that improve everything, including carving. Pivot slips are reasonable preparation for any skier who wants to learn to carve cleanly.
PSIA itself doesn't draw the progression as a single ladder; the National Standards talk about zones (beginner, intermediate, advanced, expert) and skill blends, not a fixed sequence. The boxes above are pedagogical conveniences, not ontological claims.
II.

技术进阶路线

滑雪技术通常被教成一架梯子——犁式、半犁式、平行、高级平行、carving。梯子作为初步勾勒没问题，但它在两个方向上误导：它暗示线性进阶，可实际上中级滑雪者经常在推进某些技能时退步另一些；它又暗示存在一个单一终点，可事实上梯子顶部是分叉的——carving 与挫雪小弯（短半径枢转弯）是同辈技能，谁也不从属于谁。

这张图试图说几件事：

  • 前三个阶段大致是线性的。犁式让脚知道什么叫立刃；半犁式开始引入外侧脚的主导地位；中级平行让两板协调动作。跳过其中任何一个，通常会在后面某个阶段在压力下重新冒出来当问题。
  • 高级平行是分叉点。从这里开始，技术可以朝 carving（立刃角度主导，滑板刻出干净弧线）或挫雪小弯（旋转和压力调节主导，滑板枢转刮擦雪面）方向专精。两者都依赖同样的五大基本功，只是配比不同。
  • 两个终点是同辈关系。Carving 不比挫雪小弯"更高级"。它们要求不同的地形、不同的雪。25° 压雪道上的一次刻滑是一回事；同一个滑雪者进入紧密的雪包阵线时，需要的是旋转和压力管理，而不是更大的立刃角度。把其中一个当作目标、另一个当作垫脚石，会培养出失衡的滑雪者。
  • 那条虚线反馈是真实的。挫雪小弯的训练发展压力调节与上下分离，这两件事改善所有滑雪——包括 carving。对任何想干净刻入弯的滑雪者来说，pivot slip 都是合理的准备。
PSIA 本身并不把进阶画成一架梯子；国家标准谈论的是区段（zones）——初学、中级、高级、专家——和技能配比，而不是固定序列。上面的方框是教学便利，不是本体论断言。
III.

Equipment, briefly

This section is short. Equipment selection is a deep topic with abundant external resources; the focus here is on the few geometric and material facts that genuinely affect technique.

The sidecut

A modern alpine ski is wider at the tip and tail than at the waist. Laid on edge and decambered under load, the curved edge bites into the snow as a circular arc. The radius of that arc — the sidecut radius, marked on every ski — is the natural turn the ski produces if simply tipped and released. Slalom skis sit around 12–13 m; giant slalom around 17–21 m; powder and freeride skis can reach 25 m or more.

What the sidecut tells, technically: a turn shorter than the sidecut radius requires either skidding (rotary work to scrub speed and shorten the path) or extra deformation (more edge angle, more pressure, bending the ski beyond its natural arc). A turn longer than the sidecut radius requires less than the ski wants to give — held flat, steered out, ridden through. The sidecut radius therefore divides turns into the ones the ski produces for free and the ones that have to be worked for.

Two skis of the same length can have very different sidecuts. A 175cm slalom ski (12m) and a 175cm GS ski (21m) demand different bodies, different speeds, different turn shapes. Length alone is a poor proxy for what a ski does.

Boots, more than skis

On a constrained budget, money goes further on boots than on skis. A bad ski under a competent skier is still a ski; a bad boot is a translator that drops half the message. Two numbers matter: flex index (cuff stiffness — higher numbers mean more direct power transmission and less forgiveness) and last (the foot-shape the boot is built around, expressed as forefoot width in millimeters). Stance and forward lean can be tuned by a bootfitter.

Most retail-fit skiers end up in boots that are too soft and too wide, because retail prioritizes parking-lot comfort. A boot that feels comfortable in the parking lot is usually too loose to ski well. Pain is a different matter: pain indicates a fit problem, addressable by shell punches, custom footbeds, and other targeted interventions.

III.

关于装备

本节较短。装备选择是一个深主题，外部资源充足；这里聚焦的，仅是真正影响技术的几条几何与材料事实。

侧切几何

现代高山滑雪板的板头与板尾比腰部宽。立起来后在体重下反弯（decamber），弯曲的内侧边以圆弧的形式咬入雪面。这条弧的半径就是侧切半径（sidecut radius）——每副板上都有标注——也是单纯把板立起来、放任其转动时它自然想走的弧。回转板（slalom）在 12–13 米左右；大回转（GS）17–21 米；粉雪和自由式可至 25 米以上。

侧切半径在技术上的含义是：比侧切半径更短的转弯，要么靠侧滑（旋转技术消速度、缩短路径），要么靠额外形变（更大立刃角度、更大压力，把板压弯到它自然弧之外）；比侧切半径更长的转弯则需要让板做少于它想做的——保持较平、引导出去、走过去。侧切半径因此把可能的转弯分成两类：板免费给出的，与必须做功才能换来的。

长度相同的两副板可能侧切完全不同。175cm 的回转板（12m）和 175cm 的大回转板（21m）要求不同的身体、不同的速度、不同的弯型。仅以长度判断板的性格是很差的近似。

雪靴比板更重要

预算受限的情况下，钱花在靴子上比花在板上回报更高。差板在合格滑雪者脚下仍是板；差靴是一个把信号丢掉一半的翻译。两个数字要紧：硬度指数（flex index）——筒身硬度，数值越高动力传递越直接、宽容度越低；last（楦型）——靴体所基于的脚型，以前掌宽度的毫米数表示。站姿与前倾角度可由调靴师（bootfitter）调整。

多数零售配型最后落入"过软过宽"的区间，因为零售优先考虑停车场里的舒适感。在停车场里舒服的靴子，多半已经太松，不足以滑得好。但疼痛是另一回事：疼痛意味着配型问题，调靴师可以用 shell punch、定制鞋垫等针对性手段处理。

IV.

Snow — a small taxonomy

Snow is a material that changes underfoot, sometimes within a single run. Skiing it well requires recognizing what is there, what it allows, and adjusting fundamental 5 (pressure regulation) accordingly. The classification below is a working field glossary — not meteorological taxonomy, but the categories used in practice.

Powder — fresh, dry, low-density snow
Light enough that the ski sinks until it finds resistance. Skiing it is more about flotation than edge: stance stays tall, the skis are kept closer in width, and rhythmic flexion–extension makes the skis breathe up and down through the snow. Inside-ski weight matters more here than on hardpack.
Packed Powder — groomed snow with some softness
The default Eastern morning condition. Edges hold cleanly, the ski responds, technique transfers directly from drills. Most skiers' best skiing happens here.
Hardpack & Boilerplate — dense, scraped-off, near-icy
Pressure must concentrate on a small surface area to bite. Higher edge angle, weight forward, ankle rolled hard, hands quiet. A tentative edge skids; a committed edge holds.
Ice — polished, glassy
Acceptance, then commitment. Ice cannot be bullied. Sharp edges (recently tuned) are not optional. The fall line is a friend; fighting across it loses the edge entirely.
Crud — chopped-up, refrozen, mixed
The snow that punishes inattention. The ski hits a soft pocket, then a hard rim, then a chunk. Strong stance, active flexion–extension, and forward momentum are required. Passive crud-skiing is a common mechanism of knee injury.
Corn — spring snow, granular, surface-melted-and-refrozen
The reward for skiing late in the season. Soft, forgiving, predictable. The snow grabs the edge, releases on the transition. Carving on corn is among the cleanest sensations the sport offers.
Breakable Crust — a frozen surface over softer snow underneath
The hardest snow to ski. The crust holds weight some of the time and breaks through at others, and there is no reliable way to predict which. Conservative line choice and stance discipline are the only honest answer; many advanced skiers will simply choose a different run.
IV.

雪——小型分类

雪是一种在脚下不断变化的材料，有时一道滑行之内就变好几次。把它滑好的前提，是认出当前是什么、它允许什么，并据此调整基本功 5（压力管理）。下面是一份现场分类——不是气象学的，而是实际会用到的类别。

粉雪（Powder）— 新鲜、干燥、低密度
轻到滑板要下沉一段才找到支撑。滑粉雪靠的是浮力而不是立刃：保持站立姿态、双板间距收窄一些、以节奏化的弯伸让板在雪中"呼吸"般上下。在粉雪里内侧脚的重量分配比硬雪面上更重要。
压实粉雪（Packed Powder）— 经压雪车整备且仍有柔软度
东部清晨的默认状态。立刃干净抓住，板的反馈直接，所有练习的成果都能转化进来。大多数滑雪者最好的滑行发生在这里。
硬雪 / Boilerplate— 密实、被刮过、接近冰
压力必须集中在小面积上才能咬住。更大的立刃角度、重心前移、脚踝用力内扣、双手安静。试探性的立刃会侧滑；决心的立刃才能挂住。
冰— 抛光、玻璃面
接受，然后承诺。冰不容硬碰硬。锋利的板刃（近期打磨过）不是可选项。落山线（fall line）是同盟而非对手；横切对抗将彻底丢失立刃。
糟雪（Crud）— 被切碎的、再冻结的、混合的
惩罚走神的雪。板撞进一块软窝，下一刻撞上一道硬边，再下一刻是一块大碎块。需要强健的站姿、主动的弯伸、以及让板始终向前的动量。被动地滑糟雪是膝盖受伤的常见机制。
玉米雪（Corn）— 春季雪，表面融化-再冻结过的颗粒状
滑到季末的奖赏。柔软、宽容、可预测。雪面咬住板刃，过渡时干脆释放。玉米雪上的 carving 是这项运动能给的最干净的感觉之一。
易碎冻壳（Breakable Crust）— 冻结表面下覆盖更软的雪
所有雪况里最难滑的。冻壳有时候撑得住体重，有时撑不住，且无法可靠预判。唯一诚实的答案是保守的线路选择和严格的站姿纪律；许多高级滑雪者会直接换一条道。
V.

The Mountain — a philosophy

Skiing has produced more philosophical writing per capita than almost any other sport, and not by accident. The activity sits on top of three philosophical fault lines that don't usually meet: the epistemology of bodily skill, the phenomenology of moving through a hostile and indifferent landscape, and the psychology of optimal experience. Each is treated separately below.

Tacit knowledge — Polanyi

The Hungarian-British chemist and philosopher Michael Polanyi opened his 1966 book The Tacit Dimension with a sentence that resonates throughout any practice involving bodily skill: "We can know more than we can tell." His example was bicycle riding. The textbook physical answer to "what makes a moving bicycle stable?" — the cyclist steers into the direction of fall, and the resulting curve produces a centrifugal force that arrests the fall — is true. No cyclist actually uses this knowledge. The cyclist knows how to ride. The physicist knows the equation. The two knowings do not intersect in any working bicyclist's mind.

Skiing is the same situation, magnified. A carved turn requires the simultaneous coordination of ankle inversion, knee tracking, hip angulation, fore–aft balance, and pressure regulation through the turn arc. Every component can be described — PSIA's National Standards do little else for two hundred pages — and none of those descriptions, individually or summed, will produce a carved turn in a body that has not learned it. The knowledge that skis a turn is not propositional. It is what Polanyi called tacit: a knowing-how that resists reduction to knowing-that.

Gilbert Ryle made a similar distinction in The Concept of Mind (1949): knowing how versus knowing that. Polanyi's contribution was to argue that the tacit dimension underlies even explicit knowledge — that articulation always lags what is known, and that this is structural rather than incidental.

This has consequences for teaching. Talking a student through a turn fails. Giving the student a task the body can solve — pivot slips, pole-drag turns, hop turns, javelin turns — succeeds, often without the student ever consciously understanding the principle the drill installs. Tacit knowledge is built by tasks, not by lectures.

The body-subject — Merleau-Ponty

The French phenomenologist Maurice Merleau-Ponty spent a career arguing that the body is not a thing the mind has; the body is the subject. In Phenomenology of Perception (1945) he wrote about a typist who knows where the keys are, not as facts in memory, but as locations in a body-space — the keys exist for her in a dimension that is neither her mind's nor the typewriter's, but the lived continuum between them. His most famous example, the blind man's cane, makes the same point in a different register: the cane stops being an object and becomes an extension of the body's perceptual reach.

Skis are an extreme case of the cane. After a few weeks on snow, the skier no longer feels the snow through the boot, the ski, the binding. They feel the snow at the edge — at the actual line of contact, four feet down and eight inches forward of where their physical body ends. The ski has been incorporated into the perceptual body. This isn't a metaphor; it's what Merleau-Ponty called incorporation, and it's what allows fine adjustment of edge angle without conscious thought. A novice's skis are still objects. An expert's skis are part of them.

A useful test: when a skier loses an edge, do they say "the ski slipped" or "I slipped"? The vocabulary tracks how deep the incorporation has gone.

The mountain itself is also incorporated. Experienced skiers describe terrain as having a feel — this run is "fast," that run is "moody," the bumps "want" the skier to go right. The topography has become part of the perceptual world the skier moves through, not an external object the skier reasons about. The fall line, in particular, ceases to be a geometric concept and becomes a felt direction — the way water knows downhill.

Flow — Csikszentmihalyi

The Hungarian-American psychologist Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi introduced what he originally called autotelic experience, later popularized as flow, in 1975. His earliest empirical subjects were rock climbers, chess players, and surgeons — people in challenging activities pursued for their own sake. The conditions he identified are now nearly canonical: a clear goal, immediate feedback, a balance between challenge and skill, the merging of action and awareness, and a loss of self-consciousness in the activity.

Skiing meets every condition with almost embarrassing ease. The goal is clear (get down the run, hit that line, ride that bump). Feedback is immediate and physical — the ski reports balance and edge in real time. Challenge can be precisely titrated against skill by terrain choice — a green run for the bored, a black for the over-anxious. Action and awareness merge because there is no time to separate them; a skier who is thinking about technique mid-turn is, by that fact alone, a worse skier than one who is not. Self-consciousness drops away because the ski demands too much attention to leave any over for the self.

Csikszentmihalyi was himself a skier and used the sport in many of his examples, but the original empirical work centered on rock climbers — see Beyond Boredom and Anxiety (1975).

What Csikszentmihalyi called the autotelic personality — the disposition to seek out activities done for their own sake — describes the recreational skier almost by definition. There is no extrinsic reward for skiing well. No one is watching. No one is paying. The activity is, in the Greek sense, autotelos — its own end. Flow is intrinsically rewarding because the state itself is one in which nothing is lacking.

A small synthesis

The three frames don't compete; they describe the same activity from three angles. Polanyi accounts for why skiing cannot be learned from a book — the knowledge is tacit, and tacit knowledge is built only through embodied practice. Merleau-Ponty describes what the embodied practice produces — a perceptual extension of the self into equipment and terrain, such that the skier's body reaches to the edge of the ski and to the slope under it. Csikszentmihalyi characterizes the experience when both have come off well — the merger of self and activity that is its own reward.

Underneath all three sits a fact about skiing that may be the philosophically most interesting one: it is one of the few activities in modern life in which a person voluntarily accepts the constraints of an indifferent natural force — gravity acting on a slope of crystallized water — and transforms those constraints into the medium of an art. The mountain does not care. The cold does not care. The snow does what physics tells it to. Within all of that indifference, a body learns to move with grace.

V.

山的哲学

滑雪在人均哲学产出方面，超过了几乎所有其他运动，这并非偶然。这项活动恰好坐落在三条平时不交汇的哲学断层带上：身体技能的认识论，在冷漠而充满阻力的地形里穿行的现象学，以及最优体验的心理学。下文分别处理三条。

默会知识——波兰尼

匈牙利裔英国化学家与哲学家 Michael Polanyi（迈克尔·波兰尼）在 1966 年的《默会的维度》（The Tacit Dimension）一书中以一句话开篇——这句话在任何涉及身体技能的实践中都会反复回响："我们能知道的比我们能说出的更多。"他举的例子是骑自行车。"是什么让运动中的自行车保持稳定？"教科书式的物理回答——骑车人向倾倒方向打把，所产生的曲线运动以离心力遏制倾倒——是正确的。但没有任何骑车人真的使用这套知识。骑车人知道怎么骑。物理学家知道方程。在任何会骑车的人的脑子里，两种"知道"并不相交。

滑雪是同样的情境，被放大。一次刻入的转弯，需要踝内翻、膝走线、髋折屈、前后平衡、以及在转弯弧线中调节压力的多重协调，这一切同时进行。每一项都可以被描述出来——PSIA 的国家标准用两百页只做这件事——但所有这些描述，无论单独还是合在一起，都不会让一具尚未学会的身体做出一次刻入的弯。能够滑出一次转弯的那种知识，不是命题性的。它是波兰尼所说的默会（tacit）——一种"知道如何"，拒绝被还原成"知道事实"。

吉尔伯特·赖尔（Gilbert Ryle）在《心的概念》（1949）中提出过类似区分：knowing how（知道如何） vs. knowing that（知道事实）。波兰尼的贡献是进一步主张，默会维度其实支撑着我们的所有显性知识——我们总是知道得比能讲出来得多，这是一项特征，不是缺陷。

这有实际的教学后果。试图用言语带学生走完一次转弯的教练失败了。给学生一个身体能解决的任务——pivot slip、拖杖转弯、跳跃转弯、javelin turn——的教练成功了，且学生常常从未在意识层面理解这个练习植入了什么原理。默会知识是用任务建立的，不是用讲义。

身体—主体——梅洛-庞蒂

法国现象学家 Maurice Merleau-Ponty（莫里斯·梅洛-庞蒂）用一生论证：身体不是心灵所拥有的物，身体本身就是主体。在《知觉现象学》（1945）中他写到一位打字员，她知道按键在哪里——不是作为记忆中的事实，而是作为身体空间中的位置。键盘对她而言存在于一个维度里，那个维度既不属于她的心灵，也不属于打字机，而是两者之间被活出来的连续区域。他最著名的例子——盲人的手杖——以另一种音色说同一件事：手杖不再是物，而成为身体感知的延伸。

滑雪板是手杖的极端版本。在雪上待几周之后，滑雪者不再隔着雪靴、滑板、固定器去感受雪。他们直接在板刃上感受雪——在实际的接触线上，距离他们身体物理边界四英尺以下、八英寸以前。滑板已经被纳入感知意义上的身体。这不是比喻；这就是梅洛-庞蒂所说的incorporation（合并、纳入）。也正是这种纳入，让立刃角度的细微调整可以无须意识地进行。新手的滑板仍然是物。专家的滑板是他们自己的一部分。

一个有用的测验：当一个滑雪者失去立刃，他会说"板滑了"还是"我滑了"？所用的词汇追踪着身体合并已经走多深。

山本身也是被纳入的。有经验的滑雪者会说道有"性格"——这条道"快"，那条道"阴沉"，雪包"想要"向右走。地形已成为滑雪者所穿行的感知世界的一部分，而不再是被外在地推理的对象。fall line（落山线）尤其如此：它从一个几何概念变成被感受的方向，就像水知道哪里是下坡。

心流——契克森米哈伊

匈牙利裔美国心理学家 Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi（米哈里·契克森米哈伊）在 1975 年提出了他最初称之为 autotelic experience（自成目的体验）的概念，后来以 flow（心流）之名广为流传。他最早的实证对象是攀岩者、棋手和外科医生——投身于挑战性活动、并以活动本身为目的的人。他归纳的若干条件几乎已成定论：清晰的目标、即时反馈、挑战与技能之间的平衡、行动与意识的合一、以及活动中自我意识的消退。

滑雪以近乎尴尬的轻易满足每一条。目标清晰（滑下去、走那条线、上那个雪包）。反馈即时而具身（板会实时反馈身体平衡与立刃位置）。挑战可以通过地形选择被精确滴定到当前技能——给厌烦者一条绿道，给焦虑者一条黑道。行动与意识合一，因为来不及把它们分开；正在转弯之中还在思考自己技术的滑雪者，仅凭这一点就已经是更差的滑雪者。自我意识消退，因为滑板要求的注意力多到不留余裕给自我。

契克森米哈伊本人也滑雪，并在很多例子中使用滑雪情境，但最初的实证工作集中于攀岩者——见《超越无聊与焦虑》（Beyond Boredom and Anxiety，1975）。

契克森米哈伊所说的 autotelic personality（自成目的型人格）——倾向于追求那些以自身为目的的活动的性格——几乎按定义就在描述休闲滑雪者。滑得好没有外在奖赏。没有人在看。没有人在付钱。这项活动按希腊文的本义就是 autotelos——以自身为终点。心流之所以内在地令人愉悦，正是因为这种状态本身不缺什么。

一点综合

三种框架并不竞争，它们从三个角度描述同一件事。波兰尼解释为什么滑雪不能从书里学——知识是默会的，而默会知识只能通过具身的实践被建立。梅洛-庞蒂说明这种具身实践造出来的是什么——自我向装备和地形的感知性延伸，使滑雪者的身体伸到板刃，伸到板下的山坡。契克森米哈伊刻画前两者完成得好时呈现的体验——自我与活动合一，本身即是奖赏。

三者之下还有一个事实，可能是哲学上最有意趣的一个：滑雪是现代生活里少数几种活动之一——一个人主动接受一种冷漠的自然力量所施加的限制（重力作用于一片水的结晶斜面），并把这个限制转化为一门艺术的媒介。山不在乎。冷不在乎。雪只按物理告诉它的方式行动。在这一切冷漠之中，一具身体学会了优雅地移动。

VI.

Injury & Safety

Skiing is a high-risk sport, and any honest treatment has to address that. This section covers the minimum that every skier should know: how the most common serious injury (ACL rupture) actually occurs, the agreed-upon code of conduct between skiers, and the basics of avalanche awareness for anyone leaving groomed terrain.

The ACL — the injury that defines the sport

The knee accounts for roughly a third of all skiing injuries; within that, anterior cruciate ligament rupture is the canonical one. The clinical literature identifies four main non-contact mechanisms in recreational skiing, all involving a separation of the feet and a twisting force at the knee. Modern binding standards and technique are largely organized around them.

MechanismHow it happensPrevention
Phantom Foot
flexion–internal rotation		 Skier falls backward, hips below knees, the tail of the inside ski catches and the boot's stiff back lever produces internal tibial rotation and valgus. Stay forward. Most phantom-foot falls are aft falls. The fundamental "shins on the tongue" is also the fundamental of knee preservation.
Boot-Induced Anterior Drawer Skier lands a jump back-weighted; the tail of the ski hits first, the boot back drives the tibia forward relative to the femur, and the ACL takes the load. Don't land aft. If you must jump, jump in balance, and absorb with bent knees on landing.
Valgus–External Rotation Forward fall with body rotation; the inside edge of the front of the ski catches and the lower leg externally rotates while the body continues forward. The most common mechanism since the introduction of shaped skis in the late 1990s. Active edge management. A ski that's accidentally on edge in a fall is the danger; a ski that is deliberately on edge or deliberately flat is not.
Forward Fall, No Rotation Less well-characterized; the leg loads while extending and the ACL fails through a combination of compression and quadriceps anterior drawer. General fitness, leg strength, eccentric loading capacity.
Modern bindings release on rotational and forward forces but cannot reliably release on the loads that produce phantom-foot and valgus-rotation injuries — the lever arm in those falls is often too short to trigger the binding. This is a known limitation, not a defect.

Two practical takeaways. First: nearly all the mechanisms involve being out of balance backward. Fundamental 1 — fore–aft balance, shins on the boot tongue — is therefore also the first defense against the most common serious injury in the sport. Second: when a fall is unavoidable, fighting it tends to produce more injuries than relaxing into it. Rigid quadriceps contraction is itself one of the loading mechanisms.

Your Responsibility Code

The National Ski Areas Association first published a Skier's Responsibility Code in 1962, with seven points. In 2022 it was expanded to ten and rewritten to include snowboarders, mountain bikers, and other slope users. The Code is posted at every US ski area and, where state law has incorporated it, has legal force in collision liability.

  1. Always stay in control. You must be able to stop or avoid people or objects.
  2. People ahead or downhill of you have the right-of-way. You must avoid them.
  3. Stop only where you are visible from above and do not restrict traffic.
  4. Look uphill and avoid others before starting downhill or entering a trail.
  5. You must prevent runaway equipment.
  6. Read and obey all signs, warnings, and hazard markings.
  7. Keep off closed trails and out of closed areas.
  8. You must know how, and be able to load, ride, and unload lifts safely.
  9. Do not use lifts or terrain when impaired by alcohol or drugs.
  10. If you are involved in a collision or incident, share contact information with each other and a ski area employee.

The core of the Code is rule 2 — uphill skiers are responsible for downhill skiers — because it is the only rule that cannot be inferred from common sense. Most skiers accept rules 1, 3, 4, 6, 7 instinctively. Rule 2 reverses ordinary road logic (where the trailing vehicle has right-of-way over the vehicle ahead only in narrow circumstances), and many on-mountain collisions trace back to uphill skiers who have not internalized it.

Avalanche basics — for anyone leaving the boundary

This subsection is the shortest and the most important. Skiing outside marked, patrolled boundaries — side-country, back-country, or any unmanaged terrain — requires formal avalanche education. A web page is not a substitute for an AIARE Level 1 course. What follows is orientation, not training.

Avalanche triangle Three factors must be present for an avalanche: terrain capable of producing one (slope angle of 30–45° is the danger zone), snowpack with a weak layer beneath a cohesive slab, and a trigger (most often a skier). Removing any one removes the risk.

The standard equipment for back-country travel is a transceiver (beacon), which transmits a signal so a buried skier can be located; a shovel, for digging out; and a probe, for pinpointing depth before digging. None of these are useful without practice. The North American avalanche danger scale runs through five levels — low, moderate, considerable, high, extreme — and the difference between "considerable" and "high" is, statistically, the difference between most accidents happening and most deaths happening. Local avalanche center forecasts should be read every day before going out. Lacking awareness of the local avalanche center is itself a sign of inadequate preparation for the terrain.

Helmet use deserves a note. Helmets reduce minor head injuries clearly, and reduce moderate ones somewhat; they do not protect against catastrophic high-speed impacts. The honest summary: wear one, but ski as if it weren't there.
VI.

受伤与安全

滑雪是高风险运动，任何诚实的处理都必须直面这一点。本节覆盖每一位滑雪者至少应当知道的内容：最常见的严重伤害（ACL 撕裂）的发生机制、滑雪者之间约定俗成的行为准则、以及离开整备雪道者所需的雪崩入门。

ACL——这项运动定义性的损伤

膝盖大约占滑雪损伤总数的三分之一，而在膝伤中，前交叉韧带（ACL）撕裂是经典中的经典。临床文献识别出休闲滑雪中四种主要的非接触性机制，全部涉及双脚分离与膝部扭转载荷。现代固定器标准与现代技术大体围绕它们建立。

机制怎么发生预防
Phantom Foot（幻足）
屈曲—胫骨内旋		 滑雪者向后摔，髋部低于膝盖，内侧板的尾部挂住，雪靴硬质后缘的杠杆作用产生胫骨内旋与外翻（valgus）。 保持前倾。绝大多数 phantom foot 摔倒都是后摔。基本功"胫骨顶住靴舌"也是膝盖保护的基本功。
Boot-Induced Anterior Drawer
（靴致前抽屉）		 跳跃后重心后坐着陆；板尾先触雪，雪靴后缘把胫骨相对股骨向前推，ACL 承担了这个载荷。 不要后坐着陆。如果一定要跳，要在平衡中起跳；着陆时屈膝吸收。
Valgus–External Rotation
（外翻—胫骨外旋）		 带着身体旋转的前摔；板头内侧刃挂住雪面，小腿外旋而身体继续向前。1990 年代末侧切板（shaped skis）普及之后，这是最常见的机制。 主动管理立刃。摔倒中"无意识地立着刃"才是危险，"主动立刃"或"主动放平"都不危险。
前摔，无旋转 较少被精确刻画；腿在伸直过程中加载，ACL 因压缩与股四头肌的前抽屉作用合力而失败。 总体体能、腿部力量、离心收缩能力。
现代固定器对旋转和前向力可释放，但对 phantom foot 和外旋损伤所产生的载荷往往无法可靠释放——这些摔倒中力臂过短，触发不了固定器。这是一个已知的局限性，不是缺陷。

两个实用结论。第一：几乎所有机制都涉及"重心后坐失衡"。基本功 1——前后平衡、胫骨顶靴舌——因此也是这项运动里最常见严重损伤的第一道防线。第二：当摔倒不可避免时，对抗它通常比放松进入它产生更多损伤。股四头肌的僵硬收缩本身就是其中一个加载机制。

责任准则（Your Responsibility Code）

国家滑雪场协会（NSAA）于 1962 年首次发布"滑雪者责任准则"，原本七条。2022 年扩充至十条，并改写以包括单板滑手、山地车手等其他用雪道者。这份准则张贴在美国每一个雪场；在已将其写入州法的州，它在碰撞责任认定中具有法律效力。

  1. 始终保持可控。你必须能停下或避开他人与障碍。
  2. 位于你前方或下方的人享有路权。你必须避让他们。
  3. 停留之处必须从上方可见，且不阻塞通行。
  4. 下行前或进入雪道前，先回头看上方并避让他人。
  5. 必须防止器材脱离失控。
  6. 阅读并遵守所有标识、警示、危险标记。
  7. 避开关闭的雪道与封闭区域。
  8. 必须知道并能够安全地上下与乘坐缆车。
  9. 受酒精或药物影响时不得使用缆车或地形。
  10. 若发生碰撞或事故，须与对方及雪场员工交换联系方式。

准则的核心是第二条——上方滑雪者对下方滑雪者负责——因为这是唯一无法从常识推出的条款。大多数滑雪者本能上接受 1、3、4、6、7 条。第二条颠倒了一般道路逻辑（一般道路上后车并不普遍享有对前车的路权），许多碰撞正是因为上方滑雪者没有把它内化。

雪崩入门——给所有越界者

本小节最短也最重要。在标记和巡逻的边界之外滑雪——进入 side-country、back-country 或任何无管理地形——需要正式的雪崩教育。一个网页不能替代 AIARE Level 1 课程。下面是方位标，不是培训。

雪崩三角 雪崩需要三个条件同时具备：能产生雪崩的地形（坡度 30–45° 是危险区间）；带有薄弱层、上覆有凝聚力雪板的雪层结构；以及一个触发因素（最常见是一个滑雪者）。去掉任何一项，风险即消除。

野外行进的标准装备包括：雪崩信标（transceiver/beacon），发射可用于定位被埋滑雪者的信号；雪铲，用于挖掘；探针，用于挖掘前精确定位深度。这些器材若无练习则毫无用处。北美雪崩等级分五级——low（低）、moderate（中等）、considerable（明显）、high（高）、extreme（极端）——而 considerable 与 high 之间的差别，统计上正是"大多数事故发生"与"大多数死亡发生"的差别。出门前每天都应阅读当地雪崩中心的预报。不知道所在地区的雪崩中心是哪个，本身就是越界准备不充分的标志。

头盔值得专门一提。头盔显著降低轻度头部损伤，一定程度上降低中度损伤；对高速灾难性撞击则不起保护作用。诚实的总结：戴上它，但要像没戴那样滑雪。
VII.

Teaching — field notes

Patterns observed across many students. Not technique notes — those are everywhere — but the patterns underneath the technique. Filed here as positions rather than settled views.

The plateau is psychological, not technical

Students who have skied for a few years and feel "stuck" almost never present a technical problem absent at the beginner level. What they present is a trust problem: distrust of equipment to hold an edge if the brakes are released, distrust of the body to find balance under forward commitment, distrust that the slope will behave as it has in the past. The technique is fine; the technique is throttled by caution. The work, when it works, is not on the legs but on the relationship between skier and situation. Most observed breakthroughs have been preceded by a moment of "okay, I'll try it" rather than by a new exercise.

Leaning back is fear, not laziness

It is tempting to call leaning back a technical fault. A student who is leaning back is a student whose body has decided that being far from the danger is safer than committing to it. Telling them to lean forward is asking them to overrule their own threat assessment with words. It rarely works. What works is making forward feel safer than back: terrain gentle enough to commit on, drills that produce a tip-engagement reward, a pace slow enough that the body has time to relearn. The relevant lesson is delivered to the body, not the head.

The translation problem in Chinese

Skiing terminology is overwhelmingly English. Translation into Chinese is full of small failures of precision. Angulation becomes 折屈 or 髋部弯折 or 上下身角度差 — none quite right. Edging becomes 立刃, which is fine, but 刻滑 (carving) and 立刃 are often conflated by intermediate skiers. Pressure regulation resolves to 压力调节 or 压力管理, but the connotation of touch — the fifth-fundamental sense that the snow is being read like Braille — has no equivalent in standard Chinese ski vocabulary. Teaching the English term alongside the rough Chinese rendering is the usual workaround.

The fastest learner is not the most athletic one

Athletic students often power through bad technique and lock it in; non-athletic students pay closer attention to what their body is doing. The strongest first-season learners observed have been dancers, gymnasts, and engineers — people who treat the lesson as a debugging session rather than a contest of strength.

Three things worth saying to a first-time skier

One: skiing is the only common sport in which slower is harder. Forward speed creates the dynamic environment in which the equipment works correctly. Indefinite pizza-stopping is not safer than learning to turn — it merely defers the moment of learning. Two: falling is part of the activity, not a failure of it. The relevant question is whether each fall produces a lesson or merely an absorption. Three: gravity does most of the work. The skier negotiates with it; the skier who fights it loses.

VII.

教学札记

在大量学生身上反复观察到的模式。不是技术笔记——那种东西到处都是——而是技术之下的模式。作为立场而非定论列出。

瓶颈是心理的，不是技术的

滑了几年仍感到"卡住"的学生，几乎没有谁的技术问题是初学阶段未曾出现过的。他们呈现的是信任问题：不信任装备松开刹车时仍能挂住雪刃，不信任身体在前向承诺中能找到平衡，不信任坡面会以过去的方式表现。技术是好的，技术被谨慎扼住了喉咙。这种情况下起作用的工作不在腿上，而在滑雪者与情境之间的关系上。已观察到的突破，绝大多数发生在"行，我试一下"那个瞬间之后，而不是在新练习被引入之后。

身体后仰是恐惧，不是懒

把身体后仰当作技术错误指出来很顺手。但身体后仰的学生，是身体已经决定"远离危险比委身于它更安全"。让他向前倾，是在用语言推翻他自己对威胁的评估，很少奏效。奏效的是让"向前"感觉比"向后"更安全：足够缓的地形让承诺成为可能，能产生板头反馈奖赏的练习，足够慢的节奏让身体重新学习。相关的教学是交给身体的，不是交给头脑的。

中文里的翻译问题

滑雪术语压倒性地是英文的。译入中文处处是细微的精度损失。Angulation 译成"折屈"或"髋部弯折"或"上下身角度差"——都不完全对。Edging 译成"立刃"，没问题，但中级滑雪者常把"刻滑（carving）"与"立刃"混淆。Pressure regulation 解作"压力调节"或"压力管理"，但 touch——第五基本功的那种"像在读盲文一样读雪"的意味——在标准的中文滑雪词汇里没有对应物。常见的折中是英文术语与中文近译并存。

最快的学生不是最有运动天赋的

运动型学生经常凭力量穿过糟糕的技术，把错误固化下来；非运动型学生更仔细地听自己的身体在做什么。已观察到的第一学季表现最强者，多是舞者、体操运动员、与工程师——把每节课当作 debug 而非力气较量的人。

对第一次上雪的学生值得说的三件事

一：滑雪是常见运动里唯一一项越慢越难的。向前的速度创造了让装备正确工作的动力学环境。永远 pizza-stop 并不比学转弯更安全——它只是把学的那一刻延后。二：摔是活动的一部分，不是它的失败。问题在于每次摔倒究竟产生了一个教训还是仅仅被承受了。三：重力在做大部分工作。滑雪者与它谈判；与它对抗的滑雪者会输。
