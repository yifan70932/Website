An Inexhaustible Tree

Chess as four distinct ontological objects, and what is at stake in conflating them

essay · english

The thesis of this essay is plain. Chess names not one object but four. There is a finite combinatorial game tree of roughly 10120 nodes, the object of Zermelo's 1913 theorem. There is a rule-package — a particular nineteenth-century convention that turns an otherwise infinite activity into a finite one. There is a lusory activity in Bernard Suits's sense, a structure of voluntarily-accepted constraints. And there is a fifteen-hundred-year cultural-historical practice descending from chaturanga. The four are real, none reduces to the others, and most of the philosophical confusion about chess comes from treating one as if it were another.1

1The conflation runs in all directions. Engines are said to "play chess" (collapsing the activity into the tree). Solving chess is said to "settle" the game (collapsing the cultural practice into its mathematical object). Neither inference is licit.

The argument unfolds by walking through each of the four in turn — but in an order that begins with the most concrete and ends with the most abstract, because the interesting question is whether the first is even possible as an object of human engagement once you understand the second.

1 · The cultural object

The game now called chess descends from chaturanga (Sanskrit चतुरङ्ग, "four limbs"), attested in northern India by the seventh century CE in Bāṇabhaṭṭa's Harshacharita. Its name refers to the four divisions of the classical Indian army — infantry, cavalry, elephants, chariots — which survive on the modern board as pawn, knight, bishop, and rook.2 The game travelled west into Sasanian Persia as chatrang, into the Arab world as shatranj after the seventh-century conquests, and through Moorish Spain and Sicily into medieval Europe by roughly 900 CE. It also travelled east, becoming Chinese xiangqi 象棋, Japanese shōgi 将棋, Korean janggi, and Thai makruk, each a parallel descendant.

2Murray (1913) remains the canonical history. The 8×8 ashtāpada board predates chaturanga and was used for older race games — the board was inherited; the war-simulation overlay was new.

The decisive transformation of the European game happened around 1475 in Iberia or northern Italy. Before, the queen could move only one square diagonally and the bishop could leap two squares; after the reform — Italians called it scacchi alla rabiosa, "mad chess" — both became long-range pieces and the game's tempo accelerated dramatically. Castling, en passant, and pawn promotion stabilized over the next two centuries. The fifty-move rule and threefold repetition, which we are about to see are doing crucial mathematical work, were nineteenth-century additions.3 What we mean by "chess" is therefore not a stable target across the millennium and a half of this history; it is the rule-package codified roughly 150 years ago.

3The rules were standardized internationally with the founding of FIDE in 1924; the fifty-move rule has been adjusted several times since to handle particular endgames where tablebase analysis has shown forced wins requiring more moves.

2 · The combinatorial object

Game theorists classify games along several axes. Chess sits at a particularly clean intersection: two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information, deterministic, and — under the modern rule-package — finite. This cluster is exactly the one for which classical game-theoretic theorems are sharpest. The earliest and most beautiful of those theorems, due to Ernst Zermelo, was published in 1913 in a paper called Über eine Anwendung der Mengenlehre auf die Theorie des Schachspiels, "On an application of set theory to the theory of the game of chess."4 It is now generally regarded as the founding paper of mathematical game theory.

4Schwalbe and Walker (2001) provide the canonical English translation and historical reconstruction. Their commentary corrects the persistent attribution of backward induction to Zermelo himself: Zermelo's actual proof did not use it. Backward induction is the modern reconstruction.

The theorem says that in any finite, deterministic, two-player, perfect-information, zero-sum game, exactly one of the following holds: White has a strategy that forces a win against any Black play; Black has such a strategy; or both sides can force at least a draw. The game has a determinate game-theoretic value. There is a fact of the matter about what perfect play yields, even though no human and no computer knows what that fact is.

The proof is short. Consider the game tree: a finite, branching tree where each node is a position and each edge is a legal move. By finiteness, every path terminates in a leaf labelled win-for-White, win-for-Black, or draw — say, +1, 0, or −1. Now propagate values upward by backward induction: at any node where White moves, White picks the child node with the highest value; at any node where Black moves, Black picks the lowest. By induction the root receives a unique value. That value is the game-theoretic value of chess.

The point of the demonstration is not the particular outcome at the root. The point is that the procedure is deterministic — it produces a single value for the root no matter how it is run — and conditional only on the leaf labels and the alternation of max/min. Once you accept that the tree is finite and every leaf has a determinate outcome, the value of the root is forced. That is Zermelo's theorem.

Now multiply this tree by a factor of about 10118. Claude Shannon's 1950 estimate of the chess game tree puts it at roughly 10120 nodes — the Shannon number — which dwarfs the count of atoms in the observable universe by some forty orders of magnitude. The state space (distinct legal positions, ignoring move history) is smaller, on the order of 1043–1050, but still vastly beyond any conceivable enumeration.5 Backward induction over a tree of that size is not slow; it is physically impossible.

5Shannon (1950); state-space bounds are due to Allis (1994) and refinements thereafter. Allis also formalized the typology of solved games we use below.

What chess as a combinatorial object has, then, is a game-theoretic value that is determinate but inaccessible. We know it exists; we cannot compute it. This is the precise sense in which Zermelo's theorem is a triumph of pure existence over construction. It tells us chess has an answer without telling us anything about what the answer is.

The finiteness footnote

One subtlety worth flagging: chess is finite only because of the fifty-move rule and threefold repetition. Without these stopping conventions, players could in principle shuffle pieces indefinitely, the game tree would be infinite, and Zermelo's theorem in its standard form would not directly apply.6 The determinacy of chess is therefore not an a priori fact about pieces and rules; it is a consequence of stopping conventions added in the nineteenth century. Different conventions would produce a different mathematical object. The chess of Zermelo's theorem is a particular nineteenth-century artifact, not a timeless essence.

6König (1927) and Kalmár (1928–29) extended Zermelo's argument to handle the infinite case, but only by re-imposing finiteness through other means.

Solved and unsolved

Chess is unsolved. We do not know whether perfect play yields a White win, a Black win, or a draw — though essentially every strong human and computer player believes it is a draw. But "solved" admits three senses, distinguished by Victor Allis in 1994. Ultra-weakly solved: the game-theoretic value is known but no strategy is given. Weakly solved: an algorithm plays optimally from the initial position. Strongly solved: an algorithm plays optimally from any legal position. The senses are progressively stronger; only games much smaller than chess are strongly solved.

3 · The lusory object

So far we have treated chess as a combinatorial object. But chess is also a thing people do, and game theory is silent on what playing means. The Canadian philosopher Bernard Suits, in his 1978 book The Grasshopper: Games, Life and Utopia, gave what is still the most influential analytic definition of game-playing.8 The book is structured as a Socratic dialogue between a dying Grasshopper — playful, idle, philosophically committed to play as the highest good — and his disciples. The Grasshopper proposes that game-playing has four interlocking components, three of which constitute the game and one of which constitutes the player's relation to it.

8Suits (1978/2014). The book is a sustained reply to Wittgenstein's claim in Philosophical Investigations §66 that "game" admits no definition, only a "family resemblance." Suits thinks Wittgenstein simply did not look hard enough.

The prelusory goal is a state of affairs that can be specified independently of the rules — described in terms a non-player would understand. In chess it is the geometric configuration in which one king attacks the other with no legal escape. The configuration can be reached by any number of means; one of them is playing chess.

The lusory means are the means permitted for reaching that goal. In chess they are the legal moves of each piece, the alternation of turns, the prohibition on moving into check, castling, en passant. Crucially, they are more restrictive than the means available to one who simply pursued the prelusory goal directly. You could put your opponent's king in checkmate by reaching across the board and rearranging pieces. The lusory means rule this out.

Suits's central insight is that the rules of a game are not arbitrary restrictions but constitutive ones. The lusory means do not merely tell you how to play chess after you have decided to play; they make it possible for the activity of chess to exist at all. Without the rule structure there is just a board with pieces; there is no game.

The third component is the philosophically distinctive one. The lusory attitude is the player's voluntary acceptance of the lusory means, accepted just because they make the activity possible. If your goal were simply to remove your opponent's king from the board, the most efficient means would be to reach across the table. The rules of chess prohibit this — and you, as a chess player, voluntarily accept the prohibition. You accept that you must achieve the goal in the harder way, because only the harder way constitutes playing chess. Suits's compact formulation is famous:

To play a game is to attempt to achieve a specific state of affairs, using only means permitted by rules, where the rules prohibit more efficient in favour of less efficient means, and where the rules are accepted just because they make the activity possible.9
9Suits (1978/2014, p. 54). The technical vocabulary — lusory from Latin ludus, "game" — is his coinage.

A game, in Suits's compact summary, is the voluntary attempt to overcome unnecessary obstacles. The obstacles are unnecessary because there are easier paths to the prelusory goal; they are accepted because, without them, the activity we value would not exist.

The book ends in a paradox Suits develops at length. Imagine Utopia: a world in which all instrumental needs are met, no work is necessary, and every prelusory goal achievable directly is already achieved. Suits argues that in such a world, game-playing would be the only activity left that retained any point. We would not need to do anything, but we could still take up unnecessary obstacles for the sake of overcoming them. Game-playing, on this view, is not a frivolous distraction from real life; it is the activity that survives the elimination of necessity. The Grasshopper, who plays through the summer while the Ants prepare for winter, turns out to have understood the human condition more deeply than his industrious cousins.

4 · Three confusions

With the four objects in hand — game tree, rule-package, lusory activity, cultural-historical practice — the conflations that ordinarily pass unnoticed in talk about chess become visible. Three are worth naming.

The first is the engine confusion. Stockfish does not play chess in Suits's sense. It computes optimal play within the lusory-means structure; it does not, in any meaningful sense, accept those means. Asking whether Stockfish is "really playing" chess is, on this view, a category mistake of the same kind as asking whether a calculator is "really doing" arithmetic in the way a child doing homework is. The engine is an extraordinary calculator of the lusory means; the question of whether it plays in any normatively rich sense is a different question, and Suits gives us the language to keep it separate.10

10This is not a knock on engines. The point is that "play" in the human sense and "compute optimal play" in the algorithmic sense are different relations to the same lusory-means structure.

The second is the solution confusion. If chess were strongly solved tomorrow and the answer turned out to be a draw, what would happen? Suits's framework gives a sharp answer: the prelusory goal would become unreachable for both sides under perfect play, but the lusory activity would not dissolve. People still play tic-tac-toe knowing it is a draw. The mathematical settlement of the game-tree's value is not the same event as the dissolution of the activity, because the activity exists only in players who continue to take up the lusory attitude. What would change is the cultural-historical practice — chess has been the symbolic terrain of human intelligence for half a millennium, and a proof that perfect play is a draw would alter that symbolic role even if it left the activity formally intact.11

11The disanalogy with tic-tac-toe is precisely cultural. Tic-tac-toe is so transparently solvable that no one ever invested an identity in playing it. To solve chess would be to mathematically settle a question whose openness has been culturally constitutive.

The third is the essence confusion. The Murray-Yalom genealogy that opened this essay does not link chaturanga, shatranj, fifteenth-century European chess, and the chess Stockfish computes by way of a single shared essence. They are connected by a chain of historical transmissions, each link of which involved rule changes — sometimes radical ones. The "mad queen" reform of 1475 produced a different game from the one that preceded it; the nineteenth-century stopping rules produced a different game from the seventeenth-century one; modern chess engines, in turn, are computing on a particular nineteenth-century rule-package, not on whatever Tal and Botvinnik were playing in 1960 (which was very nearly that package, but not quite). Asking what the "real" chess is reifies one moment in the chain.

5 · Back to the tree

The title of this essay names the core image. Chess, considered as a combinatorial object, is a tree of about 10120 nodes whose root has a determinate value none of us will ever compute. It is not a tree we will exhaust by playing — every chess game is a single path through it — and not a tree we will exhaust by analysis: the seven-piece tablebases occupy 18 terabytes; the eight-piece tablebases will not fit on any foreseeable disk; the full tree is many orders of magnitude beyond physical computation.

But the inexhaustibility is the wrong place to land. What matters is that this tree is only one of the four objects we have been discussing, and arguably the least important one. Players do not engage with the game tree; they engage with the lusory means under the lusory attitude. Cultures do not transmit the game tree; they transmit a rule-package and a tradition of play. When we ask, "is chess solved?" we are asking about object 1, and the answer is no, and probably will remain so. When we ask, "is chess worth playing?" we are asking about objects 3 and 4, and the answer has nothing to do with the first.

The four objects are real. None reduces to the others. The combinatorial object is what Zermelo proved a theorem about; the rule-package is what FIDE codifies; the lusory activity is what two players in a café take up across a board; the cultural-historical practice is what carried chaturanga from India to here. To call all four "chess" is convenient, but we should be honest that the word is doing four jobs, not one.
