Chess

Notes on the history, rules, mathematics, engines, and literary presence of a fifteen-hundred-year-old game

notes · english · ongoing

An ongoing collection of notes covering the history of chess, the modern rules, the game-theoretic analysis associated with Zermelo's theorem, the engine era from Turing through AlphaZero, the Elo rating system, opening theory, the philosophy of game-playing, and the place of chess in literature. Entries are added as occasion arises.

1 · History

Chess descends from chaturanga (Sanskrit चतुरङ्ग, "four limbs"), a north-Indian game attested in Bāṇabhaṭṭa's Harshacharita by the early seventh century CE. The four limbs were the divisions of the classical Indian army — infantry, cavalry, elephants, chariots — surviving on the modern board as pawn, knight, bishop, and rook. The 8×8 ashtāpada board was older still, originally used for race games; the war-simulation overlay was the new contribution.

From India the game travelled west into Sasanian Persia as chatrang, into the Arab world as shatranj after the seventh-century conquests, and through Moorish Spain and Sicily into medieval Europe by roughly 900 CE. It also travelled east, becoming Chinese xiangqi 象棋, Japanese shōgi 将棋, Korean janggi, and Thai makruk, each a parallel descendant rather than a translation.

The decisive transformation of the European game happened around 1475 in Iberia or northern Italy. Before, the queen could move only one square diagonally; the bishop could leap two squares. After the reform — Italians called it scacchi alla rabiosa, "mad chess" — both became long-range pieces and the game's tempo accelerated. The first printed chess book, Lucena's Repetición de Amores y Arte de Ajedrez (Salamanca, 1497), already used the modern moves. Castling, en passant, and pawn promotion stabilized over the next two centuries; the fifty-move rule and threefold repetition were nineteenth-century additions.

The first international tournament was held in London in 1851 (won by Adolf Anderssen). The World Chess Championship dates from the 1886 match between Wilhelm Steinitz and Johannes Zukertort in New Orleans. FIDE — the Fédération Internationale des Échecs, the game's international governing body — was founded in Paris in 1924, took over the world championship after Alekhine's death in 1946, and has codified the rules ever since.

2 · Modern rules and time controls

The basic rules are codified in the FIDE Laws of Chess, updated periodically. The board is 8×8 with alternating light and dark squares; each side begins with sixteen pieces (one king, one queen, two rooks, two bishops, two knights, eight pawns). White moves first. The game ends in checkmate, resignation, stalemate, or draw by one of several technical rules below.

Draws

Several conditions force a draw. Stalemate: the side to move has no legal move but is not in check. Threefold repetition: the same position arises three times with the same player to move and the same available moves (this is what allows perpetual check to draw). Fifty-move rule: fifty consecutive moves by each side without a pawn move or capture forces a draw on claim. Insufficient material: positions where checkmate is impossible (e.g. king vs king, king vs king and bishop). Mutual agreement: both players consent to a draw, the most common form in master play.

The fifty-move rule has a tablebase footnote. When seven-piece tablebases became available in 2012, several endgames were discovered that require more than fifty moves of perfect play to convert. FIDE has at various times granted exceptions for specific endgames (e.g. extending to seventy-five or one hundred moves for known classes of position) and at others reverted to the strict fifty-move limit. The current rule is fifty moves with no exceptions.

Time controls

FIDE classifies competitive games into four bands based on time per side, expressed as base minutes plus increment seconds per move. Increment is the modern default — invented and patented by Bobby Fischer in 1988 — and adds the specified seconds to the player's clock after each move, ensuring no game ends in a flag-fall after two hours of mutual time-pressure.

Chess960 (Fischer Random) — invented by Fischer in 1996 to defeat opening preparation, in which the back rank is randomized — has its own World Championship since 2019, alongside the recently launched Freestyle Chess events.

3 · Game theory and the tree

Chess sits at the intersection of several game-theoretic categories. It is two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information, deterministic, and — under the modern rules with stopping conventions — finite. This cluster of properties is exactly the one for which the foundational theorems of game theory hold most cleanly.

The earliest of those theorems was published by Ernst Zermelo in 1913, in a four-page paper titled Über eine Anwendung der Mengenlehre auf die Theorie des Schachspiels. Zermelo proved that in any finite, deterministic, two-player, perfect-information, zero-sum game, exactly one of the following holds: White has a strategy that forces a win against any Black play; Black has such a strategy; or both sides can force at least a draw. The game has a determinate game-theoretic value. There is a fact of the matter about what perfect play yields, even if no one knows what that fact is.

The proof reconstructs as follows. Consider the game tree: a finite, branching tree where each node is a position and each edge is a legal move. By finiteness, every path terminates in a leaf labelled +1 (White wins), 0 (draw), or −1 (Black wins). Propagate values upward by backward induction: at any node where White moves, White picks the child node with the highest value; at any node where Black moves, Black picks the lowest. By induction the root receives a unique value. That value is the game-theoretic value of chess.

The point of the demonstration is the determinacy of the procedure: it produces a single value for the root no matter how it is run, and depends only on the leaf labels and the alternation of max / min. Once the tree is finite and every leaf has a determinate outcome, the value of the root is forced.

Now multiply this tree by a factor of about 10118. Claude Shannon's 1950 estimate of the chess game tree puts it at roughly 10120 nodes — the Shannon number — which dwarfs the count of atoms in the observable universe by some forty orders of magnitude. The state space (distinct legal positions, ignoring move history) is smaller, on the order of 1043–1050, but still beyond any conceivable enumeration.1 Backward induction over a tree this size is not slow; it is physically impossible. What chess therefore has is a game-theoretic value that is determinate but inaccessible.

1Worth noting: chess is finite only because of the fifty-move and threefold repetition rules. Without them the game tree would be infinite and Zermelo's theorem in its standard form would not directly apply. The determinacy of chess is a consequence of stopping conventions added in the nineteenth century.

Solved games

"Solved" admits three senses, distinguished by Victor Allis in his 1994 thesis. Ultra-weakly solved: the game-theoretic value is known but no strategy is given. Weakly solved: an algorithm plays optimally from the initial position. Strongly solved: an algorithm plays optimally from any legal position.

Schaeffer's 2007 weak solution of checkers is the most complex result in this table. The Alberta team worked on it for nearly two decades, with up to 200 machines in parallel; the final proof combined a 39-trillion-position endgame database (built by retrograde analysis from terminal positions) with a forward proof tree from the opening.

Chess remains unsolved at the start position. Only fragments are known: the Lomonosov and Syzygy tablebases give perfect play for any position with seven or fewer pieces, and have already revealed forced mates of more than 500 moves that no human could play correctly without lookup. The longest known forced mate in the seven-piece tablebase is 549 moves (ignoring the fifty-move rule). The eight-piece tablebase is partially complete; a 2022 result includes a forced mate of 584 moves.

4 · Engines

The history of chess computing is, oddly, older than the history of computing. Alan Turing and David Champernowne wrote a chess-playing algorithm in 1948, before any machine existed that could run it. Turing later executed the program by hand in a 1952 game against his colleague Alick Glennie, taking up to half an hour per move; the algorithm — sometimes called Turochamp or "the paper machine" — lost in 29 moves, but the game is recorded and preserved. Two years earlier Claude Shannon had published Programming a Computer for Playing Chess (1950), which set out the basic minimax search and the evaluation-function framework that would dominate the next half century of engine design.

Through the 1960s and '70s engines remained weak. MIT's Mac Hack VI (Greenblatt, 1967) became the first program to compete in a human tournament, scoring a USCF rating around 1400. Belle, a custom-hardware engine built by Ken Thompson and Joe Condon at Bell Labs in the late 1970s, was the first machine to reach master strength.

The decisive moment in human–machine chess came in May 1997, when IBM's Deep Blue defeated Garry Kasparov 3.5–2.5 in a six-game match in New York. Deep Blue evaluated about 200 million positions per second using custom VLSI chess chips. The match remains controversial: Kasparov accused IBM of human intervention during the games (an accusation IBM denied), refused a rematch, and IBM dismantled the machine immediately afterward. What is uncontroversial is that after 1997, the question of whether machines could outplay the world's best humans was settled.

The two decades after Deep Blue belonged to alpha-beta engines with hand-tuned evaluation functions running on commodity hardware. Fritz, Rybka, Houdini, Komodo, and the open-source Stockfish (begun 2008, by Tord Romstad, Marco Costalba, and Joona Kiiski) traded the top of the rating lists. By the mid-2010s these engines were rated around 3300–3400 Elo, several hundred points above any human.

December 2017 brought a discontinuity. DeepMind's AlphaZero — a single neural-network architecture that learned chess, shōgi, and Go from self-play, with no opening books or endgame tables, and no human knowledge beyond the rules — defeated Stockfish 8 in a 100-game match: 28 wins, 0 losses, 72 draws, with all 28 wins as White. Training took a few hours on Google's custom TPU hardware. Grandmasters who studied the games described AlphaZero's style as "Martian" — willing to sacrifice material for long-term positional pressure in ways that no engine had previously considered viable. Garry Kasparov said it was a pleasure to watch.

The community's response was swift. Leela Chess Zero (Lc0), a distributed open-source reimplementation of AlphaZero's ideas, began training in 2018 and is now competitive with Stockfish at the top of the engine rating lists. Stockfish itself, in 2020, integrated NNUE (Efficiently Updatable Neural Network) — a small neural network used as the evaluation function inside an otherwise classical alpha-beta search. The current top engines are hybrids of the two paradigms.

An interesting consequence is that Stockfish and Lc0 still play differently. Stockfish, with its enormous search tree and crisp neural evaluation, plays a tactical and concrete style. Lc0, with its smaller search and full neural evaluation, plays in a long, positional, AlphaZero-flavoured idiom. The two engines sometimes disagree about which side stands better in a given position, and not by a small margin — a situation in which the game has a single mathematical truth, but two of the strongest evaluators on earth, looking at the same position, can return materially different verdicts.

5 · Elo and rating systems

The standard chess rating system is the work of Arpad Elo (1903–1992), a Hungarian-American physics professor and US chess master. Elo's system, designed in the 1950s as an improvement on the Harkness system then in use, assumes that each player has a true skill level that produces normally-distributed performances around it; the rating is a maximum-likelihood estimate of that skill level given the player's results. The USCF adopted it in 1960; FIDE in 1970.

The mechanics. Each game is a zero-sum exchange of rating points. Before the game, each player has an expected score based on the rating difference (a 200-point gap implies roughly 76 % for the higher-rated player; a 400-point gap implies 92 %). After the game, the actual score (1 for a win, 0.5 for a draw, 0 for a loss) is compared with the expected score; the rating moves by K times the difference. The K-factor controls volatility. FIDE currently uses K = 40 for new players (under 30 rated games), K = 20 for established players under 2400, and K = 10 for players rated 2400 and above.

FIDE rating bands are widely understood as follows: above 2700 is "Super-Grandmaster" (about 50 players in the world at any time); 2500 is the threshold for the Grandmaster (GM) title; 2400 for the International Master (IM); 2300 for FIDE Master (FM); 2200 for Candidate Master (CM). Magnus Carlsen holds the all-time peak at 2882, achieved in May 2014.

Two technical caveats are worth noting. First, ratings are relative: they measure performance against other rated players, not absolute strength. Comparing ratings across eras is fraught — a 2700 in 1972 was a different population from a 2700 today. Second, the closed-loop rating pool is in principle conservative, but inflation can creep in through new players entering with overrated provisional ratings, the rating floor, and the asymmetric K-factor. Jeff Sonas's analyses suggest mild inflation since the mid-1980s; Regan and Haworth, using engine analysis to assess move quality directly, find little inflation. The debate is ongoing.

Online platforms use variants. Chess.com runs a Glicko-style system; Lichess uses Glicko-2 (Mark Glickman, 1995, 2012), which adds a rating deviation parameter to track confidence and a volatility parameter to track recent change. Ratings on these platforms are not directly comparable with FIDE ratings — Lichess tends to run several hundred points higher than FIDE for the same player; Chess.com somewhere between.

6 · Opening theory

Opening theory is the codified knowledge of strong continuations from the starting position, organized around variations that have been played, analyzed, and scored over centuries. A typical strong player has prepared lines twenty or thirty moves deep in their main repertoire, with sidelines to handle deviations. The most widely-used taxonomy is the Encyclopaedia of Chess Openings (ECO), published in five volumes by the Yugoslav publisher Šahovski Informator from the 1960s onward; ECO codes (A00–E99) are the standard reference.

A non-exhaustive map of the major opening families:

  • Open games (1.e4 e5): Italian Game, Ruy López, Petroff Defence, King's Gambit (now rare at top level)
  • Semi-open games (1.e4, Black does not play 1...e5): Sicilian Defence (most popular Black response in master play), French Defence, Caro-Kann, Pirc, Scandinavian, Alekhine
  • Closed games (1.d4 d5): Queen's Gambit (Accepted, Declined, Slav), Catalan
  • Indian defences (1.d4 Nf6): King's Indian, Nimzo-Indian, Queen's Indian, Grünfeld, Bogo-Indian
  • Flank openings: English (1.c4), Réti (1.Nf3 d5 2.c4), King's Indian Attack
  • Hypermodern: a school of thought from the 1920s (Réti, Nimzowitsch, Tartakower) emphasizing control of the centre from the flanks rather than direct occupation

The Sicilian Defence (1.e4 c5) is the most analyzed opening in the history of chess; its Najdorf and Sveshnikov variations have been the laboratories of generations of grandmasters. The Berlin Defence to the Ruy López (1.e4 e5 2.Nf3 Nc6 3.Bb5 Nf6) was revived by Vladimir Kramnik in his 2000 World Championship match against Kasparov and has held up at the top level since, prompting some discussion about whether 1.e4 still produces a meaningful advantage at the highest levels of computer-prepared play.

Engines have shifted opening theory considerably. Many lines once thought playable have been refuted to draws or worse; conversely, lines once thought dubious (the Berlin, the Petroff) are now mainline. AlphaZero in particular preferred the Queen's Gambit and English Opening over the more popular human choices, and its games sparked a brief revival of the Queen's Indian and Catalan in elite play.

7 · Suits, lusory activity, and the philosophy of games

Bernard Suits's 1978 book The Grasshopper: Games, Life and Utopia remains the most influential analytic account of what it means to play a game. The book is structured as a Socratic dialogue between a dying Grasshopper — playful, idle, philosophically committed to play as the highest good — and his disciples. The Grasshopper proposes that game-playing has four interlocking elements.

The prelusory goal is a state of affairs that can be specified independently of the rules — described in terms a non-player would understand. In chess it is the geometric configuration in which one king is attacking the other with no legal escape. The configuration can be reached by any number of means; one of them is playing chess.

The lusory means are the means permitted for reaching that goal. In chess they are the legal moves of each piece, the alternation of turns, the prohibition on moving into check, castling, en passant. They are more restrictive than the means available to one who simply pursued the prelusory goal directly — one could put the opposing king in checkmate by reaching across the table and rearranging the pieces.

The lusory attitude is the player's voluntary acceptance of the lusory means, accepted just because they make the activity possible. Suits's compact formulation: To play a game is to attempt to achieve a specific state of affairs, using only means permitted by rules, where the rules prohibit more efficient in favour of less efficient means, and where the rules are accepted just because they make the activity possible. A game, in his summary, is the voluntary attempt to overcome unnecessary obstacles.

The fourth element is the rules themselves, which Suits takes to be constitutive rather than regulative — they do not merely tell you how to play after you have decided to play; they make the activity exist at all. Without the rules there is just a board with pieces; there is no game.

The book ends with a paradox developed at length. Imagine Utopia: a world where every instrumental need is met. Suits argues that in such a world, game-playing would be the only activity left that retained any point — we would not need to do anything, but we could still take up unnecessary obstacles for the sake of overcoming them. Game-playing, on this view, is the activity that survives the elimination of necessity. The Grasshopper, who plays through the summer while the Ants prepare for winter, turns out to have understood the human condition better than his industrious cousins.

Suits's framework gives precise language for a recurring confusion about chess. Stockfish does not "play" chess in his sense — it computes optimal moves within the lusory-means structure, but does not accept the structure. The distinction is not available in the language of game theory by itself.

8 · Chess in literature and film

Chess is one of the most over-used metaphors in writing about thinking, war, love, and politics — and a recurring subject in serious fiction. A short, partial canon:

Vladimir Nabokov's The Defense (Защита Лужина, 1930) follows the Russian grandmaster Aleksandr Luzhin from his isolated childhood through his career and breakdown. Nabokov was himself a strong amateur and a serious chess problem composer; the novel's chess content is technically precise. Stefan Zweig's Schachnovelle (The Royal Game, 1942 — published the year of Zweig's suicide) is a novella set on a transatlantic ship, where a man who learned chess in solitary confinement under the Gestapo finds himself playing the world champion. It is widely regarded as one of the most important short fictions about chess in any language.

Lewis Carroll's Through the Looking-Glass (1871) is structured as a chess game, with Alice as a pawn on the second rank promoting to a queen on the eighth. The moves are listed in the front matter; many of them are not legal, but the structural conceit is the point.

Walter Tevis's The Queen's Gambit (1983) was largely overlooked at publication and became a worldwide phenomenon thirty-seven years later through the 2020 Netflix adaptation, which prompted a measurable global increase in chess set sales and online tournament registrations. Bruce Pandolfini consulted on the book; Garry Kasparov on the series. Ingmar Bergman's Det sjunde inseglet (The Seventh Seal, 1957), in which the knight Antonius Block plays chess with Death on a Swedish beach, is the source of much of the subsequent "playing chess with Death" iconography in Western culture.

Other notable appearances of chess in literature: Jorge Luis Borges's poem "Ajedrez" treats the chess pieces as a metaphor for the player as himself a piece in a larger game, played by a god who is in turn a piece in some larger game still. Italo Calvino's Invisible Cities (1972) closes with a scene in which Marco Polo and Kublai Khan read their conversations into the marquetry of an empty chessboard. Vikram Seth's The Golden Gate (1986) and Elena Ferrante's The Days of Abandonment (2002) each contain a substantial chess scene.

9 · Notes on tournaments and titles

The World Chess Championship has been held since 1886. Champions in roughly the modern era: Botvinnik (1948–1963, with interruptions), Petrosian (1963–1969), Spassky (1969–1972), Fischer (1972–1975, who refused to defend), Karpov (1975–1985, by default), Kasparov (1985–2000), Kramnik (2000–2007), Anand (2007–2013), Carlsen (2013–2023, voluntarily relinquished), Ding Liren (2023–2024), Gukesh Dommaraju (2024–present). Carlsen's withdrawal — he said he no longer felt sufficient motivation to defend — was a significant moment. Ding's title win and Gukesh's subsequent victory at age 18 marked the entry of the championship into a post-Carlsen era.

The Candidates Tournament selects the challenger; format has varied (knockout, double round-robin, currently an eight-player double round-robin). The FIDE World Cup (knockout, 128 or 206 players) has run roughly biennially. The Chess Olympiad is a biennial team event; the Tata Steel Chess Tournament in Wijk aan Zee (Netherlands, since 1938) is the longest-running invitational super-tournament.

Major titles, in descending order: Grandmaster (GM, 2500+ with three norms), International Master (IM, 2400+), FIDE Master (FM, 2300+), Candidate Master (CM, 2200+). Women's titles (WGM, WIM, WFM, WCM) sit 200 points lower; titled women are not separately ranked from men in the open lists.

10 · Personal notes

On checkmate, not development

One observation, after a fair amount of play and engine review: most losses at the club level come from prioritizing piece development and material preservation over the actual goal of the game, which is to mate the opposing king. Stockfish review tends to confirm this — the moments where the evaluation swings against the losing side are usually moments where one player keeps maneuvering pieces into "good squares" while the other player is already coordinating an attack on the king.

This connects to a point worth recalling from §7 above: in Suits's analysis, the prelusory goal of chess is the geometric configuration of the opposing king under attack with no legal escape. Material is not the goal. Position is not the goal. The king is the goal. Suits's framework is sometimes accused of being too abstract; this is a case in which it gives a very practical reminder.

Personal preference, derived from this observation: trade pieces aggressively and head into the endgame as soon as the resulting position is no worse than equal. Endgames have a higher ratio of "what does the king actually need to do" to "where do these pieces want to be," which suits the stripped-down logic of the prelusory goal.

11 · Further reading and resources

Histories: Murray's A History of Chess (1913) is encyclopedic and unsurpassed in scope, though English-Asian transmission accounts have been refined since. Yalom's Birth of the Chess Queen (2004) tracks the queen piece — its rise, the 1475 reform, and the larger cultural symbolism — through a thousand years of European history.

Game theory: Schwalbe and Walker's Zermelo and the early history of game theory (Games and Economic Behavior, 2001) is the canonical reconstruction of Zermelo 1913 and includes the standard English translation. Allis's PhD thesis (Limburg, 1994) is the source for the typology of solved games. Schaeffer et al.'s Checkers Is Solved (Science, 2007) is the best technical writing on a real solving project.

Engines and AI: Silver et al.'s AlphaZero papers (2017 preprint and 2018 Science paper) are accessible reading. Matthew Sadler and Natasha Regan's Game Changer (2019) is the strongest book-length engagement with AlphaZero's playing style by working chess professionals. Behind Deep Blue by Feng-hsiung Hsu (2002) is the inside account of the 1997 match.

Practical play and resources: Lichess.org is free, open source, and has the best free training tools in existence (puzzle racer, Studies, opening explorer, the seven-piece tablebase API). The Saint Louis Chess Club's YouTube channel and chess24 archives contain decades of grandmaster commentary. ChessBase software (commercial) is the standard for opening preparation at a serious level. For pure problem-solving and the aesthetic of the chess composition tradition, the Yacpdb chess problem database and the journals The Problemist and EG are unmatched.

Suits and the philosophy of games: The Grasshopper (1978; Broadview reissue 2014, with introduction by Thomas Hurka) is the primary text. C. Thi Nguyen's Games: Agency as Art (2020) extends Suits in directions that are productive and disagreeable in roughly equal measure.

Literature: Nabokov's The Defense (English translation by Michael Scammell with the author, 1964) and Zweig's Schachnovelle (any of several English translations) are the two essentials. Walter Tevis's The Queen's Gambit (Vintage reissue 2020) is worth reading even after the series.
