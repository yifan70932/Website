What Is Chess?

History, game theory, Zermelo's theorem, and the lusory ontology of a 1500-year-old game

essay · english

Chess is the game most often used as a metaphor for thinking. It stands in for war, for politics, for love, for the pursuit of intelligence itself. But chess is also a mathematical object — a finite, deterministic, perfect-information, two-player, zero-sum game — and one of the few cultural artifacts that has been simultaneously a popular pastime, a research instrument in artificial intelligence, the subject of a foundational theorem in mathematical logic, and a recurring example in the philosophy of action.1 What follows is an attempt to take all of these registers seriously at once.

1This essay is not a history of the game in the chronicler's sense. For that, see Murray (1913), still unsurpassed in scope.

1 · A short history

The game we now call chess descends from chaturanga (Sanskrit चतुरङ्ग, "four limbs"), a game attested in northern India by the sixth or seventh century CE.2 Its name refers to the four divisions of the classical Indian army — infantry, cavalry, elephants, chariots — which survive today as pawn, knight, bishop, and rook. The earliest unambiguous reference is in Bāṇabhaṭṭa's Harshacharita, written around 625 CE, which praises the peace of King Harsha's reign with the line "only ashtāpadas teach the position of the chaturanga."

2The 8×8 ashtāpada board predates chaturanga and was used for older race games. Whether chaturanga was ever a four-player dice game, as some traditions claim, is debated.

From India the game travelled west into Sasanian Persia as chatrang, then to the Arab world as shatranj after the Islamic conquest of Persia in the seventh century, then onward through Moorish al-Andalus and the Emirate of Sicily into medieval Europe by the tenth century. A separate northern route — through the Volga–Caspian trade and Byzantine contact — carried the game to Kievan Rus' by the ninth century, and Viking traders eventually took it as far as Iceland and the Hebrides, where the famous Lewis chessmen were buried in the twelfth century.3

3The Lewis chessmen, ninety-three pieces of walrus ivory found on the Isle of Lewis in 1831, are usually attributed to Norwegian workshops c. 1150–1200. They depict the bishop already as a figure with a mitre — evidence that by then the piece had been thoroughly Christianized.

Chaturanga also went east, where it became Chinese xiangqi 象棋, Japanese shōgi 将棋, Korean janggi 장기, Thai makruk, and others — each branch adapting to local military and aesthetic traditions. The relation between these eastern variants and their Indian ancestor is partly genealogical and partly convergent; chess, in the broadest sense, is a family of games rather than a single object.

The decisive transformation of the European game happened around 1475, in either Spain or Italy. Before this reform the queen could move only one square diagonally; the bishop, only two squares diagonally and could leap. After the reform they became long-range pieces, and the game accelerated dramatically. Italian players called it scacchi alla rabiosa — "mad chess." Murray (1913) and Yalom (2004) link the queen's empowerment to the contemporaneous rise of powerful European queens such as Isabella of Castile.4 Castling, en passant, and pawn promotion stabilized over the next two centuries. The fifty-move rule and threefold repetition — which, as we shall see, are doing crucial mathematical work — were nineteenth-century additions, codified to ensure that games actually terminate.

4The thesis is suggestive but contested. Material conditions — the printing press, growing leisure among urban elites — likely mattered as much as any single political symbol.

2 · Chess as a game-theoretic object

Game theorists classify games along several axes. Chess sits at a particularly clean intersection of them. It is two-player: only White and Black act. It is zero-sum: one player's gain is precisely the other's loss; there is no possibility of joint benefit. It is of perfect information: every legal position, every prior move, every option available to either player is fully known to both. There is no hidden hand of cards, no fog of war, no concealed pieces. It is deterministic: no dice, no shuffling, no stochastic element. And, with the modern stopping rules, it is finite: the game must terminate after a bounded number of moves.5

5This last point is more subtle than it sounds. Without the fifty-move rule and threefold repetition, chess admits infinite play and is therefore not strictly finite. We return to this below.

This unusual cluster — finite, deterministic, perfect-information, two-player, zero-sum — is exactly the cluster for which the central theorems of classical game theory are sharpest. It is the reason chess has served, since the early twentieth century, as a touchstone case for thinking about strategy in the abstract.

3 · Zermelo's theorem

In 1912, at the Fifth International Congress of Mathematicians in Cambridge, Ernst Zermelo — best known today as a foundational figure in axiomatic set theory — gave a talk on chess. The following year he published Über eine Anwendung der Mengenlehre auf die Theorie des Schachspiels ("On an application of set theory to the theory of the game of chess"), which is now generally regarded as the first paper in mathematical game theory.6

6Schwalbe and Walker (2001) provide the canonical English translation and historical reconstruction. Their commentary corrects several persistent misreadings of the original argument.

The theorem, in its modern formulation, says: in any finite, deterministic, two-player, perfect-information, zero-sum game, exactly one of the following must be true.

  1. White has a strategy that forces a win against any Black play.
  2. Black has a strategy that forces a win against any White play.
  3. Both sides have a strategy that forces at least a draw.

In other words, the game has a determinate game-theoretic value. There is a fact of the matter about what perfect play yields, even though no human and no computer knows what that fact is.7

7The theorem is purely existential. It guarantees that a winning or drawing strategy exists; it gives no way of finding one.

The proof is short and beautiful. Consider the game tree: a finite, branching tree where each node is a position and each edge is a legal move. By the assumption of finiteness, every path from the root terminates in a leaf labelled win-for-White, win-for-Black, or draw. Now propagate values upward by backward induction: at any node where it is White's turn, White will choose the child node with the value most favourable to White; at any node where it is Black's turn, Black will choose the child most favourable to Black. By induction the root receives a unique value.8

8One subtle correction: Zermelo's own 1913 proof did not in fact use backward induction in the form now standard. The backward-induction reading was retroactively imposed by later authors. See Schwalbe & Walker (2001).

The theorem is a triumph of pure existence: it tells us chess has an answer without telling us anything about what the answer is. The size of the game tree is what makes the result inert in practice. Claude Shannon estimated the number of distinct chess games at roughly 10120 — the so-called Shannon number — which dwarfs the number of atoms in the observable universe by some forty orders of magnitude. The set of legal positions is smaller, on the order of 1043–1050, but still vastly beyond exhaustive computation.9 Backward induction over a tree of that size is not slow; it is physically impossible.

9Shannon (1950), "Programming a Computer for Playing Chess." The 10120 figure is an estimate of game-tree size; state-space estimates are due to subsequent work by Allis (1994) and others.

The finiteness footnote

There is a wrinkle. Without the fifty-move rule (a draw if no pawn moves and no capture occur in fifty consecutive moves) and threefold repetition (a draw if the same position arises three times), chess is not a finite game. Players could in principle shuffle pieces back and forth indefinitely. Without these stopping rules, Zermelo's theorem in its standard form does not directly apply.10

10König (1927) and Kalmár (1928–29) extended Zermelo's argument to handle this case, but only by re-imposing finiteness through other means. Strictly speaking, "chess" without modern stopping rules is an infinite game.

What this means is that the determinacy of chess is not an a priori metaphysical fact about the game's pieces and rules; it is a consequence of stopping conventions added in the nineteenth century. Different conventions would yield a different mathematical object. The "chess" of Zermelo's theorem is thus a particular nineteenth-century rule-package, not the timeless essence of the activity.

4 · Solved games and the typology of solution

Chess is unsolved. We do not know whether perfect play yields a White win, a Black win, or a draw — though essentially every strong human and computer player believes the answer is a draw. But "solved" is not a single concept. The Dutch computer scientist Victor Allis distinguished three senses in his 1994 dissertation, and the typology has since become standard.11

11Allis (1994), Searching for Solutions in Games and Artificial Intelligence. PhD thesis, University of Limburg.
  • Ultra-weakly solved: the game-theoretic value of the initial position is known. We know who wins under perfect play, but not how.
  • Weakly solved: an algorithm is known that, from the initial position, plays out an optimal line and achieves the game-theoretic value against any opponent.
  • Strongly solved: an algorithm is known that plays optimally from any legal position, including positions that arise after one or both players have made mistakes.

The three senses are progressively stronger. Ultra-weak solutions can sometimes be obtained by clever non-constructive argument — strategy-stealing, parity considerations, and similar. Weak solutions almost always require massive computation. Strong solutions require the entire state space to be evaluated.

The Schaeffer et al. result on checkers is worth pausing over. Their team at the University of Alberta worked on the proof for nearly two decades, with up to 200 machines running in parallel. The final published result, in Science in 2007, weakly solved the game from the standard opening: with perfect play on both sides, English draughts is a draw. The proof combined a 39-trillion-position endgame database (computed backwards from terminal positions) with a forward proof tree from the opening.12 It is currently the most complex game ever solved.

12Schaeffer, Burch, Björnsson, Kishimoto, Müller, Lake, Lu, & Sutphen (2007), "Checkers Is Solved," Science 317, 1518–1522.

For chess, only fragments are solved. The seven-piece endgame tablebases — the Lomonosov tablebase, completed in 2012, and the more compact Syzygy tablebases that followed — give perfect play for any position with seven or fewer pieces on the board. They have already revealed surprises: positions previously thought to be theoretical draws turn out to be wins requiring more than five hundred moves of perfect play, far beyond any human capacity. Whether this counts as "knowing chess" is a question we leave open.13

13The longest known forced mate in the 7-piece tablebase is 549 moves (ignoring the fifty-move rule). The 8-piece tablebase is partially complete; the longest forced mate found there as of 2022 is 584 moves. Both are positions no human could play correctly without tablebase access.

5 · The Suitsian view: chess as a lusory activity

So far we have treated chess as a mathematical object. But chess is also a thing people do, and game theory is silent on what it means to play. The Canadian philosopher Bernard Suits, in his 1978 book The Grasshopper: Games, Life and Utopia, gave what is still the most influential analytic definition of game-playing.14

14Suits (1978/2014). Wittgenstein had famously argued in the Philosophical Investigations §66 that "game" admits no definition, only a "family resemblance." Suits's book is, among other things, a sustained reply: he thinks Wittgenstein simply did not look hard enough.

The book is structured as a Socratic dialogue between a dying Grasshopper — playful, idle, philosophically committed to play as the highest good — and his disciples. The Grasshopper proposes that to play a game is to engage in a particular kind of activity, and he isolates four interlocking elements. Three of them define what a game is; the fourth, the lusory attitude, distinguishes playing a game from merely producing the same outcome by other means.

The prelusory goal

The prelusory goal is a state of affairs that can be specified independently of the rules of the game — described in terms a non-player would understand. In chess, the prelusory goal is the geometric configuration in which one player's king is attacking the other's king with no legal escape. You can describe this configuration without saying anything about whose turn it is, or what counts as a legal move; it is just a pattern of pieces on a board. The prelusory goal in golf is having one's ball come to rest in a hole. In a footrace, it is being the first to cross a particular line. The point is that prelusory goals can in principle be achieved by means other than the game.

The lusory means

The lusory means are the means permitted for achieving the prelusory goal. In chess, these are the legal moves of each piece, the alternation of turns, the prohibition on moving into check, the rules of castling and en passant. Crucially, the lusory means are more restrictive than the means available to one who simply pursued the prelusory goal directly. You could put your opponent's king in checkmate by reaching across the board and rearranging pieces, or by hypnotizing them into resigning, or by a thousand other routes. The lusory means rule these out.

Suits's central insight is that the rules of a game are not arbitrary restrictions; they are constitutive restrictions. The lusory means do not merely tell you how to play chess after you have decided to play; they make it possible for the activity of chess to exist at all. Without the rule structure there is just a board with pieces — there is no game.

The lusory attitude

The third element is the philosophically distinctive one. The lusory attitude is the player's voluntary acceptance of the lusory means — accepted just because they make the activity possible. This is what Suits calls the "psychological element," and it is what distinguishes a game from mere goal-pursuit.

If your goal were simply to remove your opponent's king from the board, the most efficient means would be to reach across and pick it up. The rules of chess prohibit this — and you, as a chess player, voluntarily accept the prohibition. You accept that you must achieve the goal in the harder way, because only the harder way constitutes playing chess. Suits's compact formulation is famous:

To play a game is to attempt to achieve a specific state of affairs, using only means permitted by rules, where the rules prohibit more efficient in favour of less efficient means, and where the rules are accepted just because they make the activity possible.15
15Suits (1978/2014, p. 54). Suits coins the term lusory from Latin ludus, "game" — hence "lusory goal," "lusory means," "lusory attitude." The whole technical vocabulary is his invention.

Putting the four elements together: a game is the voluntary attempt to overcome unnecessary obstacles. The obstacles are unnecessary because there are easier ways to reach the prelusory goal; they are accepted because, without them, the activity that we value — the game — would not exist.

The Grasshopper's paradox

The book ends in a paradox that Suits develops at length. Imagine Utopia: a world in which all instrumental needs are met, no work is necessary, and every prelusory goal achievable directly is already achieved. Suits argues that in such a world, game-playing would be the only activity left that retained any point. We would not need to do anything, but we could still take up unnecessary obstacles for the sake of overcoming them. Game-playing, on this view, is not a frivolous distraction from real life; it is the activity that survives the elimination of necessity. The Grasshopper, who plays through the summer while the Ants prepare for winter, turns out to have understood the human condition more deeply than his industrious cousins.16

16The argument is more delicate than my summary suggests. Suits is careful to distinguish games from other voluntary activities (art, friendship, contemplation), and the Utopia argument is meant to show that games would be central, not exhaustive, in such a world.

Where this lands chess

Applying this triad to chess brings several things into focus that the game-theoretic picture cannot see.

First, chess as a game-theoretic object and chess as a lusory activity are not the same thing. The mathematical chess of Zermelo's theorem is fully specified by its prelusory goal and its lusory means — the win condition and the legal-move structure. But it has no players, no acceptance, no attitude. It is a tree, not an activity. The activity exists only when someone takes up the lusory attitude toward that tree.

Second, this clarifies what computer chess engines are doing. Stockfish does not play chess in Suits's sense. It computes optimal play within the lusory means; it does not, in any meaningful sense, accept those means. Asking whether Stockfish is "really playing" chess is, on this view, a category mistake of the same kind as asking whether a calculator is "really doing" arithmetic in the way a child doing homework is.17

17This is not a knock on engines. They are extraordinary calculators of the lusory-means structure. But the question of whether they play in any normatively rich sense is a different question, and Suits gives us the language to keep it separate.

Third, the existence of solved games puts pressure on the lusory attitude in an interesting way. If we strongly solve chess and the result is a draw, the prelusory goal becomes — under perfect play — unreachable for both sides. Does this dissolve the game? Suits would say no: the activity remains, because the players still accept the rules and engage with the structure they constitute. People still play tic-tac-toe, after all, knowing it is a draw. But the relation between solvedness and meaningful play is genuinely interesting: it is part of what makes the prospect of solving chess emotionally fraught for the chess world in a way the prospect of solving Connect Four was not.18

18The disanalogy: tic-tac-toe is so transparently solvable that no one ever invested an identity in playing it. Chess has been the symbolic terrain of human intelligence for half a millennium. To solve it would be to mathematically settle a question whose openness has been culturally constitutive.

6 · Coda: what kind of object is chess?

We are now in a position to answer the title question, though only by refusing the singular. Chess is at least four distinct kinds of object, and confusion arises whenever we treat one as if it were the others.

  1. A finite combinatorial game tree, of size roughly 10120, whose game-theoretic value is determinate by Zermelo's theorem but unknown.
  2. A rule-package — a particular nineteenth-century convention with stopping rules — that turns the otherwise infinite game into the finite one.
  3. A lusory activity in Suits's sense: a structure of rules taken up by players who voluntarily accept its restrictions in order to make the activity possible.
  4. A cultural-historical practice with a continuous tradition from sixth-century India, transformed by the fifteenth-century European reform, sustained by clubs and federations and players whose engagement gives the previous three layers their meaning.

Each of these is real. None of them is reducible to the others. The mathematical chess of Zermelo and Shannon is not the chess that Tal played against Botvinnik in 1960; that chess is not the chess that Stockfish computes in 2026; and none of those is identical to the chaturanga from which they all descend. What unites them is something like a Wittgensteinian family resemblance — though Suits, as we noted, would say it is rather a shared structure of prelusory goal, lusory means, and lusory attitude, instantiated in different histories by different beings with different relations to the rules.

Whether one prefers Wittgenstein's resemblance or Suits's structural definition is itself a substantive philosophical choice. But on either reading, "What is chess?" is not a single question. It is at least four, and any answer that pretends otherwise is doing more violence to the game than the game deserves.
